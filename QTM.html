Toward a Quality Theory of Money

In honor of Friedman and Snider

Abstract

While traditional monetary theories focus on quantity—how much money exists, how fast it circulates—this proposes a complementary frame: a quality theory of money. Reframing money as a communication protocol, not a commodity, this theory centers around what kind of information money encodes, how well it travels, and what systems it sustains.

1. Introduction: From Quantity to Quality

The Quantity Theory of Money (QTM) has long served as orthodoxy: MV = PQ. But this describes volume, not value. What if money is not merely a fluid, but a medium of meaning?

“Inflation is not just too much money chasing too few goods—it’s too much noise drowning out the truth.”

2. The Four Pillars of Monetary Quality

2.1 Signal Integrity

Money is a message. Poor-quality money garbles the message. High-quality systems preserve signal clarity between producers, consumers, and capital allocators.

2.2 Transmission Trust

Money must be a trusted timestamp—verifiable, accepted, and resistant to coercion. Trust is infrastructural, not just institutional.

2.3 Friction and Latency

Friction (fees, delays) and latency (policy lag) are monetary drag. Quality money is efficient without sacrificing accountability.

2.4 Reflexivity and Feedback

Systems must sense themselves. Money should be self-correcting, with real-time data and adaptive feedback loops.

3. Comparative Analysis

Property High-Quality Money Low-Quality Money
Signal Integrity Transparent, traceable, coherent Opaque, distorted, manipulated
Transmission Trust Broad trust, legal finality Localized trust, brittle credibility
Friction & Latency Fast, efficient, accountable Slow, costly, error-prone
Reflexivity & Feedback Self-correcting, measurable Self-reinforcing, blind

4. Implications for Policy and Design

  • Central Banks: Focus on informational clarity, not balance sheet size.
  • Digital Currencies: Evaluate by auditability and interoperability.
  • Alternative Systems: Map against these quality metrics, not ideology.
  • Trust is measurable: Develop epistemic and systemic robustness indices.

5. Conclusion: From Friedman to the Future

Milton Friedman showed us that money matters. Jeff Snider reminds us that systems matter more. Quality theory says:

Money is not simply a unit—it is a medium of meaning. Its quality is our collective economic intelligence.
